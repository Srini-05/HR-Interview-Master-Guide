Basic Introduction

01 Tell me about yourself
Common
Sample Answer

I'm a software developer with around 2.5 years of experience, primarily working with React, Node.js, and REST APIs. I completed my B.Tech in Computer Science in 2022 and joined [Company Name] as a junior developer, where I've grown into handling end-to-end feature development.

I recently led a module to optimize our dashboard load time by 40% using lazy loading and caching — that's the kind of performance-focused work I really enjoy.

Outside of coding, I enjoy reading about system design and contributing to open-source projects. I'm now looking for an opportunity where I can take on more ownership and grow into a mid-level or senior role.

💡 Keep it under 90 seconds. End with why you're here today.

05 Why are you looking for a job change?
Critical
Sample Answer

I've had a great experience at my current company and learned a lot. However, I feel I've reached a point where the work has become repetitive and I'm not being stretched technically as much as I'd like.

I'm looking for a role that exposes me to more complex problems — whether that's higher scale, a different architecture, or a more product-focused team where my work directly impacts users.

I want my next role to be a step up, not just a step sideways. This opportunity stood out because of [specific reason].
💡 Never badmouth your current employer. Focus on growth, not escape.
06 Why should we hire you?
Critical
Sample Answer

I bring a combination of solid technical skills and a strong sense of ownership. I don't just write code — I think about the product, the user, and the long-term maintainability of what I build.

In my current role, I've delivered features end-to-end — from requirement gathering to deployment — with minimal hand-holding. That kind of self-sufficiency is something I can bring here on day one.

On top of that, I'm a quick learner. When my team adopted a new CI/CD pipeline last year, I took the lead in setting it up because no one else had prior experience. I enjoy those challenges.

07 What are your career goals?
Growth

Longer term, I'm interested in either a technical lead or architect path, depending on where my strengths take me. I care deeply about building scalable systems and I'd like to develop deeper expertise there.

💡 Align your goals with what this role can offer. Show ambition + realism.

10 What is your biggest professional achievement?
Showcase
Sample Answer (STAR Format)

Situation: Our main dashboard was loading in 6–8 seconds, causing drop-offs and user complaints.

Task: I was asked to investigate and improve the performance without a full rewrite.

Action: I profiled the app, identified heavy synchronous API calls and un-memoized components, implemented lazy loading, split API calls, and added Redis caching on the backend.

Result: Load time dropped from 7 seconds to under 2 seconds — a 70% improvement. User retention on that page improved by 25% the following month.
💡 Always quantify results. Numbers make stories credible.
11 Tell me about a challenge you faced and how you solved it
STAR
Sample Answer

We had a critical bug in production right before a major client demo — a race condition in our payment flow that only appeared under concurrent load.

I stayed back with the team, reproduced it in a local environment using load simulation, traced it to a missing database transaction lock, and pushed the fix after proper testing — all within 4 hours.

The demo went ahead without issues. After that, we introduced better concurrency testing to our pipeline to catch such issues earlier.
12 Describe a situation where you handled pressure well
Behavioral
Sample Answer

During a sprint, a senior teammate fell ill two days before a deadline and I was asked to pick up their incomplete tasks on top of mine.

Instead of panicking, I quickly triaged the tasks — separating must-haves from nice-to-haves — communicated transparently with my manager about what was realistic, and focused on delivering the core functionality first.

We shipped the critical parts on time. The remaining non-blocking items were moved to the next sprint with proper context handed off. My manager appreciated the transparency and decision-making.
13 Tell me about a time you failed
Honest
Sample Answer

Early in my career, I underestimated the complexity of an API integration task and gave an overly optimistic timeline. Midway through, I realized there were undocumented edge cases that would take much longer to handle.

I informed my team late — which caused some planning disruption. The lesson I took from this was to always add buffer time for unknowns and to flag blockers early, even if it's uncomfortable.

Since then, I've developed a habit of breaking tasks into smaller spikes before committing to estimates, and I proactively over-communicate progress.
💡 Interviewers want honesty + reflection + growth. All three matter.

Skills & Behavior

5 questions
14 What are your strengths?
Classic
Sample Answer

My biggest strength is ownership mindset — once I take on a task, I see it through completely and take responsibility for the outcome, not just the code.

I'm also good at debugging and root cause analysis. I genuinely enjoy tracing through systems to understand why something broke, rather than applying quick patches.

And lastly, I'm a clear communicator — I can explain technical issues to non-technical stakeholders in a way that's actionable and not jargon-heavy.

15 What are your weaknesses?
Classic
Sample Answer

Honestly, I tend to over-engineer solutions sometimes — I get excited about making things "perfect" when "good enough" would ship faster. I've been actively working on this by setting time-boxes for design decisions and reminding myself that iteration beats perfection.

I've also been working on becoming more comfortable speaking up in large group discussions — I tend to think before I speak, which sometimes means I miss the moment. I've been improving by jotting down key points before meetings.

💡 Be genuine. Then show you're actively improving it.
16 How do you handle feedback?
Behavior
Sample Answer

I genuinely welcome feedback — I see it as a faster path to improvement than figuring things out alone. When I get feedback during code reviews, I first try to understand the reasoning behind it before implementing the change, because that's how I actually learn and not just fix.

Early in my career, a senior dev called out that my functions were doing too many things. Instead of defending myself, I asked him to walk me through it. That conversation fundamentally changed how I approach function design.
17 How do you manage deadlines?
Behavior
Sample Answer

I break the work down into daily milestones so I always know whether I'm on track or not. If I hit a blocker that threatens the deadline, I raise it early — not the day before, but as soon as I see the risk.

I also protect focus time by batching meetings and async communication so I have uninterrupted coding blocks. That discipline has helped me consistently meet commitments without burnout.

18 How do you prioritize tasks when everything feels urgent?
Behavior
Sample Answer

I use a simple impact-vs-effort matrix. I ask: which of these tasks blocks someone else or directly affects the product / user? Those go first. Then I assess effort — high-impact, low-effort items get done immediately.

If priorities are truly unclear, I sync with my manager rather than guessing, because wrong prioritization is more expensive than a 10-minute conversation.

Team & Communication

3 questions
19 How do you handle conflicts in a team?
Team
Sample Answer

My first step is to make sure both sides actually understand each other's position — most technical conflicts stem from different contexts or assumptions, not fundamental disagreements.

I try to move the conversation from "I think X is right" to "here's the tradeoff between X and Y, given our constraints." Making it about data and tradeoffs rather than opinion reduces ego involvement significantly.

Once we had a disagreement on REST vs GraphQL for a new API. Instead of debating endlessly, I proposed a brief spike on both — we built small prototypes and the team aligned based on concrete results.
20 Have you ever disagreed with a teammate or manager?
Honesty
Sample Answer

Yes. I once disagreed with a decision to skip writing tests for a feature due to time pressure. I shared my concern, explained the risk clearly, and proposed a middle ground — write tests only for the critical paths. My manager agreed, and those tests caught a regression a month later.

I believe it's important to disagree respectfully but clearly, and then commit to the final decision wholeheartedly once it's made — even if it wasn't mine.

21 How would you describe your communication style?
Soft Skill
Sample Answer

I'd describe my style as direct, but empathetic. I prefer written communication for complex topics — it forces clarity and creates a record — but I default to a quick sync for anything that involves ambiguity or emotion.

I also make a conscious effort to adapt based on audience: technical detail for engineers, outcome-focus for product and business stakeholders.

Tech

24 What would you do if you don't know something?
Honesty
Sample Answer

I say so clearly and then figure it out. I'm not afraid to admit knowledge gaps — pretending to know something and delivering something broken is far worse.

My typical approach: I'd tell my team I'm unfamiliar with it, do a focused 1–2 hour research session, and come back with either a solution or a clear question. I also reach out to colleagues who have experience in that area — the best developers I know are excellent at leveraging the knowledge around them.

NegotiationCritical

27 Are you open to negotiation?
Negotiation
Sample Answer

Yes, absolutely. I believe every offer has room for a thoughtful conversation. If the base is lower than I expected, I'd want to understand what the variable component looks like, what the review cycle is, and whether there are ESOPs or other benefits that could bridge the gap.

I won't negotiate just for the sake of it — but I will advocate clearly for what I believe is fair, backed by market data. I'm also happy to sign quickly once we've agreed, so there's no dragged-out process on my end.
💡 Always counter-offer at least once. Most offers have 10–20% flex built in.

32 Do you have any questions for us?
Must Ask
Smart Questions to Ask
  • What does a typical onboarding look like for developers joining the team?
  • What are the biggest technical challenges the team is facing right now?
  • How does the team handle technical debt vs. new features?
  • What does career growth look like for developers in this company?
  • How are engineering decisions made — top-down or collaborative?
  • What does success look like for someone in this role in the first 6 months?
Never say "No, I'm good." Always ask at least 2 questions. It signals genuine interest and curiosity.
💡 Avoid asking about salary or WFH policy in the HR round unless they bring it up.
